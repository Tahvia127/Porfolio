Behind the Grade

Analyzing behavioral and background factors influencing student academic performance: How partitioned regression revealed that study habits work differently for high and low performers

DATA 119 Final Project • 6,607 Students • R² = 0.968

Overview

How do students' behavior and background characteristics influence their academic performance? Our project investigated factors such as study hours, sleep patterns, attendance, and motivation levels as predictors of final exam scores. Using the "Student Performance Factors" dataset from Kaggle, we analyzed 6,607 students across 20 variables with exam scores ranging from 57% to 101%.

Inspired by a 2023 New York Times article examining the effectiveness of virtual learning environments, we sought to test how behavioral and demographic factors correlate with academic success. Our analysis revealed that relationships between variables and outcomes are not uniform across all performance levels, leading us to develop a partitioned regression approach that dramatically improved prediction accuracy.

The Study Hours Paradox

For students scoring below 77%, each additional hour of study led to an average 1.78% increase in exam scores. However, for high-performing students above 77%, additional study time correlated with a 3.1% decrease in final exam scores. This counterintuitive finding suggests high performers may benefit more from efficiency and quality over quantity, while struggling students need the extended exposure.

Key Findings

Attendance Drives Success Universally

Regardless of performance level, regular classroom attendance significantly improved exam outcomes. Low-performing students saw an average 2.32% score increase for each 1% attendance increase, while high performers saw 2.64% gains. This aligned with research showing physical presence in learning environments as critical to academic success.

Previous Performance Compounds

Past academic achievement was among the strongest predictors of future success. Low performers gained 0.7% on their final exam for each additional point on previous exams, while high performers gained 1.54%, suggesting academic success builds cumulatively and early interventions have outsized long-term impacts.

Partitioned Model Dramatically Outperforms Single Model

Our initial single regression model achieved R² = 0.7198, performing well for students below 80% but deviating significantly for high performers. After experimenting with thresholds between 77% and 81%, we found 77% optimal for partitioning. The combined partitioned model achieved R² = 0.968, a 34% improvement.

Motivation, Teacher Quality, and Past Scores Predict High Performance

Logistic regression revealed that Motivation Level, Teacher Quality, and Previous Scores were the most important factors in predicting whether students would score above 77%. Students with higher motivation, better teachers, and stronger past scores were consistently more likely to excel on future exams.

Methodology

Data Preparation and Cleaning

Addressed missing values using appropriate imputation: filled Teacher Quality with mode values, and created "Missing" categories for Parental Education Level and Distance from Home. Standardized categorical variables and converted them to numerical form using ordinal encoding for ranked features and dummy encoding for nominal variables.

LASSO Regularization

Applied LASSO with alpha = 0.01 determined through cross-validation to reduce multicollinearity and filter weak predictors. Successfully removed Physical Activity as a less significant predictor while retaining features with non-zero coefficients.

Initial Multiple Linear Regression

Fit regression model on entire dataset achieving R² = 0.7198. Discovered model performed well below 80% but predictions deviated significantly for high scorers, motivating exploration of fundamental differences between performance groups.

Partitioned Regression Approach

Experimented with thresholds between 77% and 81%, determining 77% as optimal cutoff. Trained separate models for students above and below threshold, combining predictions to achieve final R² = 0.968.

Logistic Regression Classification

Applied logistic regression to categorize students above/below 77%. ROC curve yielded AUC = 0.393. Model achieved precision of 0.995 for identifying low performers but only 0.007 for high performers, demonstrating classification difficulty between groups.
