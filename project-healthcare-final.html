Healthcare Payment Analysis:
Following the Money in Women's Health

Analyzing pharmaceutical payments to OB/GYN specialists from 2017–2020, revealing how policy changes and COVID-19 transformed industry spending patterns.

Overview

What does $157M in pharma payments reveal about women's health?

The Open Payments Database, maintained by the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS), was created under the Physician Payments Sunshine Act of 2010 to promote transparency in financial relationships between healthcare providers and industry companies. This analysis examines $157,768,067 in payments to OB/GYN specialists across 1,175,177 transactions from 2017-2020.

During this period, significant healthcare policy changes and economic factors influenced payment distributions. The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act shifted financial priorities in the healthcare sector. In 2020, the COVID-19 pandemic caused a dramatic decline in medical procedures and pharmaceutical sponsorships, while the increasing adoption of value-based care models encouraged shifts in funding structures.

COVID-19 Impact: 43% Payment Decline

Payments dropped from $46.45M in 2019 to $26.30M in 2020, a 43% decline. Transactions fell from 344,400 to 166,605. This dramatic reduction reflected the suspension of in-person physician meetings, cancellation of medical conferences, and the healthcare industry's shift to crisis response mode during the pandemic.

Key Findings

Reproductive Endocrinology: 140% Surge in Average Payments

Counter to overall trends, Reproductive Endocrinology saw average payments increase from $305.63 per transaction in 2017 to $734.75 in 2020, a 140% increase. This surge reflects growing pharmaceutical investment in fertility treatments and assisted reproductive technologies, driven by new-generation fertility drugs and expansion of fertility preservation laws in multiple states.

Quarterly Financial Cycles Drive Seasonal Patterns

Between 2017-2019, monthly transactions consistently peaked in March and October, suggesting industry payments were linked to quarterly financial cycles. In 2020, this pattern disrupted with spikes in April and May due to emergency relief funds directed toward healthcare providers during the early pandemic months.

Geographic Disparities: California vs Wyoming

In 2019, California received $5,332,767.53 while Wyoming received only $3,269.42. These disparities reflect population differences, healthcare infrastructure concentration, and pharmaceutical company targeting strategies. California, Texas, and Florida led in transactions due to extensive medical institutions and active pharmaceutical collaborations.

Steady Growth Through 2019, Then Pandemic Collapse

Payments increased steadily from $41.92M in 2017 to $46.45M in 2019, reflecting growing investment in medical research and clinical partnerships. However, the 2019 Drug Pricing Transparency Initiative increased scrutiny over pharmaceutical payments, potentially influencing certain transaction declines before the pandemic's dramatic impact in 2020.

Policy Context Timeline

2010: Physician Payments Sunshine Act

Created the Open Payments Database under the Affordable Care Act to promote transparency in financial relationships between healthcare providers and industry companies. This foundational legislation enabled the analysis of pharmaceutical payment patterns.

2017: Tax Cuts and Jobs Act

Repealed the individual mandate of the Affordable Care Act, resulting in decreased insured patients and shifting financial priorities in the healthcare sector. Corporate tax reductions provided pharmaceutical companies with additional capital, potentially contributing to payment increases observed in 2017-2018.

2019: Drug Pricing Transparency Initiative

Increased scrutiny of pharmaceutical pricing and physician relationships influenced company spending strategies. While payment levels remained elevated through 2019, this policy shift may have contributed to changes in certain transaction types before the pandemic.

2020: COVID-19 Pandemic

The pandemic fundamentally disrupted pharmaceutical-physician engagement. April and May 2020 experienced a 70% drop in transactions compared to previous years, corresponding with hospital shutdowns, canceled elective procedures, and reduced pharmaceutical industry marketing activities. Total payments fell 43% as companies shifted focus toward emergency medical responses.

Value-Based Care Transition

The increasing adoption of value-based care models encouraged shifts in funding structures, as healthcare providers were incentivized to focus on patient outcomes rather than volume-based services. States with established telehealth frameworks maintained more stable industry transactions compared to those with limited digital health infrastructure.
