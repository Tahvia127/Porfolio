Forgotten Wars

Quantifying the attention hierarchy of global conflict: How Western political salience, not human suffering, determines which wars the world cares about

DATA 23700 Final Project · 884,216 Events · 11 Countries

Research Question

How strongly do documented conflict events correlate with Google search volume for conflict-related terms, and how does this relationship vary across crises? This investigation tests whether public attention follows humanitarian severity or operates according to other factors like Western political salience, geographic proximity, or media framing.

Using ACLED's comprehensive conflict database and Google Trends search data, this analysis examined 884,216 conflict events across 11 countries from 2020-2025, revealing systematic patterns in how global attention is allocated to human suffering.

Core Finding

ATTENTION = f(Western Political Salience) NOT f(Human Suffering). Analysis reveals that correlation between documented violence and public search interest ranges from r = 0.77 (Palestine) to r = 0.03 (Yemen), a 25-fold difference that has nothing to do with death toll. Yemen's 165,446 deaths generate r = 0.03 correlation while USA's 496 deaths generate r = 0.75 correlation — a 416:1 death ratio with inverse attention patterns.

Key Findings

Western Salience Determines Visibility

Conflicts with clear Western strategic interests maintain strong correlations between violence and attention. Palestine (r=0.77), USA (r=0.75), and Ukraine achieve moderate-to-strong correlations. Meanwhile, conflicts without direct Western involvement — Syria (r=0.16), Yemen (r=0.03), Ethiopia — fade into statistical invisibility regardless of humanitarian severity.

Syria: The Forgotten War

Despite 145,292 events and 141,446 deaths from 2020-2025, Syria generates almost no public attention (r=0.16). After dominating headlines during 2016-2018, Syria became normalized as a permanent crisis. The world learned to look away, demonstrating how sustained violence creates attention fatigue rather than sustained concern.

The Death Toll Paradox

More deaths do not predict more attention. Yemen experienced 165,446 fatalities with correlation of r=0.03. Ukraine saw 217,249 deaths with moderate correlation. The USA's 496 deaths achieved r=0.75. This inverse relationship proves attention follows political salience, not humanitarian need.

Attention Decay Patterns

Even geopolitically significant conflicts experience exponential attention decay. For Ukraine, search interest dropped dramatically after the initial invasion despite hundreds of weekly deaths continuing. The half-life of conflict attention appears to be approximately 3 months, after which events must be dramatically escalatory to recapture public focus.

Implications for Humanitarian Funding

This research demonstrates that attention allocation follows predictable patterns based on Western political priorities rather than humanitarian need, creating "forgotten wars" that remain systematically underfunded despite ongoing violence. Syria, Yemen, and Ethiopia receive minimal international aid relative to casualty counts.

Methodology

ACLED Data Collection

Processed weekly aggregated data from ACLED's comprehensive database of political violence and protests worldwide, covering 884,216 events with precise geolocated details, dates, violence types, and fatality numbers across 6 global regions (Africa, Asia-Pacific, Europe-Central-Asia, Latin America-Caribbean, Middle East, US-Canada).

Google Trends Integration

Collected normalized search interest (0-100 scale) for 44 calibrated conflict-related terms for each country. Terms were carefully selected to capture different aspects of public attention while avoiding bias toward specific narratives or perspectives.

Time-Lagged Correlation Analysis

Implemented Pearson correlation coefficients to quantify relationships between conflict events and search volume. Time-lagged analysis revealed predominantly reactive attention patterns, with searches following rather than anticipating violence escalations.

Cross-Country Comparison

Analyzed 11 countries representing diverse conflict types, geographic regions, and levels of Western political salience. Countries included Ukraine, Syria, Yemen, Palestine, India, USA, Myanmar, Ethiopia, and others, enabling identification of systematic attention allocation patterns.

Pattern Classification

Conflicts were categorized into attention tiers based on correlation strength: High Western Salience (r > 0.6), Moderate Salience (r = 0.3-0.6), Low Salience (r = 0.1-0.3), and Forgotten Wars (r < 0.1). This classification revealed clear dividing lines based on geopolitical factors.
