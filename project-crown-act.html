More Than Hair:
A CROWN Act Evaluation

An intersectional policy evaluation of the CROWN Act and its real-world impact on Black women in the workplace — combining legal analysis, the scholarly record on hair bias, and original interviews with Black women across multiple industries.

Course

PBPL 28498 · Women and Society

Institution

Harris School of Public Policy

Term

Spring 2026

Overview

A law that names the problem isn't the same as a law that solves it.

Twenty-eight states and D.C. have enacted versions of the CROWN Act since California passed SB 188 in 2019. The law expands the legal definition of race to include hair texture and protective styles, closing a gap left open by the 2016 EEOC v. Catastrophe Management Solutions ruling. Yet 80% of Black women still report feeling pressure to alter their hair for work — even in states with full protection. This evaluation asks why.

What the project does

  • Maps the policy: the actors, incentives, and political economy of the CROWN Coalition, state legislators, and employer resistance.
  • Synthesizes the literature on hair bias across psychology, law, sociology, and public health.
  • Conducts original interviews with two Black women navigating workplaces in Ohio, Illinois, Georgia, Indiana, and the skilled trades.
  • Applies course frameworks: colonial beauty standards, the gap between formal law and informal norms, and the role of biased data in obscuring gendered discrimination.
  • Closes with five concrete recommendations — federal passage, standardized complaint tracking, mandatory employer training, public education campaigns, and explicitly intersectional policy design.
"You can tell when there's a situation where I change my hair... all of the feedback I'll receive is, 'Oh my goodness, I really like your hair.' And it's only when they're in those, what would be considered to be more tamed or manageable states." — interview, professional working across four states

Frameworks applied

  • Crenshaw (1989) — intersectionality as the lens for why a race-only framing leaves a gendered problem partially unsolved.
  • Higginbotham (1993) — respectability politics as the mechanism by which informal pressure substitutes for explicit rules.
  • Opie & Phillips (2015) — the empirical "agency penalty" applied to Afrocentric hair, including by Black evaluators.
  • Geyton et al. (2026) — the measurement gap: no study has directly tested the CROWN Act's mental-health impact.
